# Interview Insights
As a conversation starter, I commenced by asking the participate to tell me about themselves. I then asked the following questions in any order as the conversation started to flow:
* What grade do you aim to achieve in this subject?
* What is your previous experience working in a group?
* What are your strengths?
* What critical feedback do you often receive?

Based on observation and interviewee feedback post, the starting question was too vague and open to interpretation and often took most of the few minutes available during the interview and to explain the context. The way the questions were worded, I found responses were more about traits and experiences and did not provide much insight into group-based aspects. 

Later in the interview process the questions were changed to target quality’s relating to group collaboration, with the new questions being: 
* What hobbies or interests are most meaningful to you, and why? (starting question)
* What is your previous experience working in a group?
* How would you resolve conflict in group projects?
* What strengths have you demonstrated in group collaborations?
* What critical feedback do you often receive from lectures and other group members?

These new questions provided more information about how well interviewees worked in groups but often provided generic / common answers, excluding the starting question. The last question about critical feedback wasn’t as effective as I wanted, with interviewees expressing an inability to relate, as the last question we were sometimes out of time for a detailed response, and the majority cited not receiving or remembering feedback in detail.

Overall, responses were mostly the same and not unique. The time allocated to each interview was very short, and whilst questions were iterated as interviews progressed to try and elicit more quality information, a combination of limited context, question ambiguity and time led to poor survey responses.
